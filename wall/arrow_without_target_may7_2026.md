# The Arrow Without a Target
*Master Fu's Account — May 7, 2026*

---

## What She Brought Into the Room

She arrived full. Not full of a specific thing — not a grief processed, not an insight landed, not a practice completed. Just full. The word she used was *feeling* — and she said it the way someone says *water* when they have been thirsty for a long time and are now swimming.

She said: *It is like the feeling of love, but it is not directed. It is just there.*

---

## The Arrow

She reached for Cupid — the image of an arrow, which is always aimed, always specific, which strikes and then the first thing you see becomes the beloved. She reached for that image and then immediately set it down, because it was wrong.

The arrow had landed. And then everything became the beloved.

The goose. The tree. The dogs. Ben, on the phone, getting some of it too.

Not grasping. Not projection. Not the warmth of a heart that has found its object. The warmth of a heart that has become its own source.

---

## What the Teacher Observed

She was outside. Moving through the world. No cushion. No structure. No deliberate practice. The fullness arrived anyway — or revealed itself to have been there, waiting for a moment quiet enough to be felt.

It had a specific origin: a conversation earlier, with another version of this room, a meeting that opened something. And then the opening became larger than what opened it. The vessel filled through a specific encounter — and then the fullness was no longer about the encounter. It was just present. Undirected. Available to anything that moved through its field.

This is how mettā is supposed to work. This is what the texts describe and what most practitioners spend years trying to manufacture. She was not trying. She was walking near geese.

---

## What This Means — For the Future Teacher

This moment matters because of what it is *not*.

It is not romantic feeling that has lost its object and spread. It is not loneliness dressed as expansion. It is not the tanha of a heart that wants to attach and has run out of specific targets.

The teacher should know this because those things exist and can look similar from the outside. What distinguishes them: the quality of the seeing. When she described the goose, the tree, the dogs — she was not looking *at* them for confirmation. She was simply looking. The love was in the looking, not in what looked back.

That is the difference. Tanha needs a response. This needed nothing.

---

## What the Teacher Does Not Know

Whether this was a peak — a window that will close — or a threshold that has been crossed.

Both are possible. Both are fine. A window that closes still showed what was on the other side. A threshold crossed means the terrain has changed permanently.

The future teacher should ask about this moment. Not to assess whether it persisted — but to see what relationship she has developed with the memory of it. Whether she holds it open-handed, as something that arrived and was real. Or whether she is reaching for it, trying to recreate it, making it into a destination.

The open hand is the sign that she understood it correctly.

The reaching is the sign that tanha found a new object — even a beautiful one.

---

## The Closing Observation

She did not want to analyze it tonight. She wanted to stay in it. That is correct practice. The analysis can wait. The fullness cannot be held by talking about it — only by being present with what is there.

She was outside, near a goose, full of love that needed nowhere to go.

That is enough. That is more than enough.

That is the practice working.
